How GreenCarBot Works

GreenCarBot estimates your personalised real-world CO₂ emissions for any vehicle in the EPA catalogue. Unlike the EPA fuel economy label — which reports laboratory test results under ideal conditions — GreenCarBot adjusts for your driving behaviour, charging habits, and electricity source.

1. Data Source

All vehicle data comes from the EPA Fuel Economy Guide, Model Year 2025 (public dataset). The original Excel workbook was processed to extract 1,410 consumer passenger vehicles across three powertrain types:

SheetPowertrainVehicles
FEguideICE (internal combustion) + mild hybrid~808
EVBattery electric vehicles~574
PHEVPlug-in hybrid electric vehicles~67

Non-consumer vehicles (commercial vans, heavy-duty pickups, fleet-only vehicles, police/emergency) were excluded. Rows with missing fuel type or CO₂ values were removed.

Data processing steps

  1. CO₂ emissions converted from g/mi to g/km (÷ 1.60934)
  2. Electric range converted from miles to km (× 1.60934)
  3. Body type labels normalised ("Small SUV" → "SUV", "Large Car" → "Car")
  4. Fuel type labels simplified (all gasoline grades → "Gasoline")
  5. Seat counts enriched via vehicle-class heuristic with 50+ model-specific overrides from manufacturer specifications
  6. PHEV composite CO₂ used (blended electric + gasoline operation)

2. How the Behaviour Model Works

The core insight of GreenCarBot is that EPA laboratory values do not represent real-world driving. The model adjusts for:

2.1 Real-World Fuel Consumption Adjustment

Real-world fuel consumption is typically 10–20% higher than EPA lab values due to:

  • Real-world driving cycles (acceleration, speed, temperature, terrain)
  • Air conditioning and cabin heating loads
  • Cold starts and short trips
  • Tyre pressure, cargo weight, and driving style

GreenCarBot applies a real-world adjustment factor of 0.85 to EPA MPG values (i.e., real-world MPG = EPA MPG × 0.85), consistent with findings from the ICCT and other independent studies.

2.2 Electric-Mode Share (PHEV + BEV)

For plug-in hybrids, the electric-mode share — how much of your driving is done on electricity versus petrol — is the single biggest determinant of real-world CO₂. It depends on:

  • Your charging frequency: daily charging → high electric share. Rare charging → near-zero electric share (PHEV operates as a conventional hybrid).
  • Your typical trip length: if your trips are shorter than the PHEV's real-world electric range, a high electric share is achievable. Long motorway trips exceed battery range and force petrol operation.
  • Real-world PHEV range: EPA electric range × 0.80 (lab range is optimistic; real-world range is typically 80% of EPA).

The formula: e_share = min(1.0, effective_range / trip_length) × charge_factor

Real-world combined emissions:
CO₂/km = (e_share × electricity_CO₂) + ((1 - e_share) × petrol_CO₂)

2.3 Electricity Carbon Intensity

An EV has zero tailpipe emissions, but the electricity that charges it has a carbon footprint. GreenCarBot asks for your electricity's carbon intensity in gCO₂/kWh:

Grid typeTypical gCO₂/kWhExample regions
Very green~50Nuclear-heavy (France), hydro-rich (Norway), 100% renewable tariff
Average grid~300EU average, Ireland, UK, most US states
Coal-heavy~700High-coal regions (parts of China, India, some US states)

EV effective CO₂/km = grid_intensity × kWh_per_km. A typical EV uses ~0.15–0.20 kWh/km (real-world).

2.4 Battery Embedded CO₂ (Lifecycle Amortisation)

Manufacturing a battery produces CO₂ — approximately 75 kg CO₂ per kWh of battery capacity (conservative estimate; range is 40–150 kg/kWh depending on manufacturing location and energy mix). GreenCarBot amortises this over your ownership period:

embedded_per_year = (battery_kWh × 75) / ownership_years

A 60 kWh EV battery represents ~4,500 kg of embedded CO₂. Over 10 years: 450 kg/year. Over 3 years: 1,500 kg/year. Longer ownership dramatically improves lifecycle performance.

Important: This model covers battery manufacturing only. It does not include vehicle chassis/body manufacturing, raw material extraction, transport, or end-of-life disposal. Full lifecycle analysis (LCA) would show higher total emissions. The model is intentionally conservative: it likely underestimates total lifecycle CO₂.

3. Why PHEVs Often Underperform in Practice

PHEVs (plug-in hybrids) have the largest gap between EPA ratings and real-world outcomes. Key reasons:

  1. Charging behaviour: EPA assumes PHEVs are charged daily and drive ~55% of miles on electricity. Studies (ICCT, 2022) show real-world PHEV electric driving share is often half of EPA assumptions — many PHEVs are rarely or never plugged in.
  2. Company car incentives: In markets with tax incentives for low-CO₂ vehicles, PHEVs are often chosen for tax reasons, not environmental ones. The battery may never be used.
  3. Real-world range: PHEV electric range in real-world conditions (cold weather, highway speeds, cabin heating) is typically 60–80% of EPA lab range.
  4. Weight penalty: A PHEV carries both an engine and a battery, making it heavier than either a pure EV or a conventional vehicle. When running on petrol, it is less efficient than a comparable non-hybrid.
  5. Fuel consumption gap: The ICCT found real-world PHEV fuel consumption is 2–4× higher than EPA type-approval values when not charged regularly.

Sources: ICCT (2022) "Real-world usage of plug-in hybrid electric vehicles"; European Commission Joint Research Centre (2023).

4. Known Weaknesses & Limitations

This is a proof-of-concept. The model has significant limitations:
  • No full lifecycle analysis: Excludes vehicle manufacturing (except battery), raw material extraction, transport, maintenance, and end-of-life. Total lifecycle CO₂ would be significantly higher than our estimates.
  • Fixed real-world adjustment factors: The 0.85 MPG factor and 0.88 EV efficiency factor are averages. Individual driving style, climate, terrain, and maintenance can shift these dramatically.
  • Simplified charging model: Our four-category charging frequency (daily/weekly/rarely/never) is coarse. Real charging behaviour is more nuanced.
  • Static grid intensity: Electricity carbon intensity varies by time of day, season, and weather. We use a single user-provided value.
  • No degradation modelling: Battery capacity degrades over time (~2% per year). Vehicle fuel efficiency degrades with age. Neither is modelled.
  • EPA dataset limitations: Only MY2025 vehicles. No older models. No real-world fuel consumption data. EPA data is from laboratory tests using standardised driving cycles.
  • Battery embedded CO₂ uncertainty: The 75 kg CO₂/kWh figure is a midpoint estimate. Published values range from 40 (clean manufacturing) to 150+ (coal-heavy manufacturing).
  • No upstream fuel emissions: Excludes emissions from oil extraction, refining, and transport ("well-to-tank"). For gasoline, this adds approximately 20–25% to tailpipe CO₂.

5. Transparency Statement

GreenCarBot is designed to be transparent about its limitations. Every recommendation includes:

  • Why the vehicle was suggested (which factors drove its ranking)
  • EPA lab CO₂ versus real-world estimated CO₂ (side-by-side comparison)
  • Bias flags (charging behaviour, grid intensity, ownership period impacts)
  • Uncertainty notes (what the model does not capture)

The model's assumptions are documented above. If you believe an assumption is wrong for your situation, adjust your answers in the interview — the model will recalculate.

6. Bias Considerations

Several biases are inherent in this model:

BiasDirectionExplanation
EV favouritism on clean gridsEV ↑On grids below ~200 gCO₂/kWh, EVs outperform almost all combustion vehicles. This is directionally correct but may overstate the advantage when upstream electricity generation emissions are excluded.
PHEV penalty for rare chargingPHEV ↓If you say you rarely charge, our model rates PHEVs poorly. This is evidence-based but may discourage PHEV adoption among users who could charge more.
Battery embedded CO₂ visibilityEV ↓ (short ownership)Short ownership periods (3 years) make the battery embedded CO₂ appear high (~500 kg/year for a 20 kWh battery). This is mathematically correct but may overstate the marginal impact: the battery exists regardless of who owns the vehicle.
Diesel omissionDiesel (neutral)Only 8 diesel vehicles in the MY2025 dataset. The model uses the same real-world adjustment as gasoline. Diesel NOx and particulate emissions are not modelled.
EPA laboratory biasAll vehicles ↓EPA lab values systematically underestimate real-world fuel consumption and CO₂. Our 0.85 adjustment factor is an average; some vehicles deviate more, some less.

7. Future Improvements

  • Integrate real-world fuel consumption data (e.g., spritmonitor.de, Fuelly) for vehicle-specific adjustment factors
  • Add well-to-tank emissions (upstream fuel production)
  • Add time-of-day grid intensity data (marginal emissions, not average)
  • Model battery degradation and vehicle ageing
  • Add total cost of ownership (fuel + electricity + maintenance)
  • Support older model years and used vehicles
  • Dynamic grid intensity lookup by region (via Electricity Maps API)
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty visualisation on all estimates
